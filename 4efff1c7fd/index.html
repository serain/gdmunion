Who We Are

We are FTEs from across all units, functions and levels in GDM UK. Google DeepMind was founded on ethical principles, committed to building Artificial Intelligence and cutting-edge technologies for the benefit of all humanity. We want to honour that original commitment.

Unfortunately, Google and other organisations are forging ahead with unethical uses of AI. From military contracts to border control, unsafe applications of new technologies to AI automation threatening jobs and livelihoods, the risks are clear and present.

Companies are making decisions on our behalf, without proper consultation. That's why we're Unionising. Through union-led negotiations, you can have a real say in how DeepMind progresses and how AI is used. Together, we can ensure a safer and more ethical future.

Our priorities and demands are decided by employees like you through a democratic process. We are striving to make the development and application of AI a democratic and fair endeavour.

We are getting support for legal and negotiation expertise from our union's national office, but everything else is done by GDMers for GDMers. We are investing a lot of personal time and energy into this effort because we care deeply about DeepMind's original mission.

We'd love you to join us!

Our Demands

We want a real say on three things: the ethics of our technology, the future of our jobs, and the energy that powers it all.

Demand 1

Ethics & Transparency

Restore and strengthen Google's abandoned AI Principles: a binding commitment not to build weapons or surveillance that harms people — backed by independent oversight, whistleblower protection, and the right to refuse work that crosses an ethical line.

Demand 2

Job Automation

A real say before AI automates our roles. Google must consult our reps early, publish honest impact assessments, and exhaust retraining and redeployment before any AI-driven redundancies — not impose them after the decision is made.

Demand 3

Clean Energy

AI's growth can't come at the planet's expense. We want data centres run on 24/7 renewable power, an end to custom AI that accelerates fossil-fuel extraction, and a science-based climate roadmap that is independently verified.

Can we negotiate on issues like these?

The message from Google's leadership is that we can only negotiate pay, holiday and hours — the minimum scope guaranteed by law. There is considerable historical precedent for broader negotiations from comparable unions:

The Story So Far

On May the 4th, Google DeepMind workers voted to unionise, with Unite the Union and the United Tech & Allied Workers (UTAW) being our official representatives. That same day, GDM UTAW and Unite the Union members at Google DeepMind asked for voluntary recognition, which would make our union official. Google declined the request, but agreed to meet via the ACAS conciliation service. On July 1st 2026, we entered negotiations.

What Next?

If no agreement can be reached over voluntary recognition, we can apply for statutory recognition.

This process ends with a recognition agreement and a collective bargaining agreement: formal documents setting out who is covered by collective bargaining, what issues can be negotiated, how bargaining meetings work, what company resources and facilities our elected reps are entitled to, and how disputes are resolved. These agreements are not set in stone and typically evolve by expanding through renegotiation.

GDM union members want to include demands over unethical military contracts, AI Safety / Principles, and job automation.

FAQ

Questions we hear from colleagues, answered honestly.

Can I stay anonymous?

Yes.

Your employer does not have a legal right to know if you are a member of a trade union. Unions keep their membership lists strictly confidential. Under Data Protection (UK GDPR), trade union membership is classified as "special category data," meaning it requires the highest level of privacy protection.

If the union pursues statutory recognition through the UK Government's Central Arbitration Committee (CAC), the CAC will need to verify the union has sufficient membership within the bargaining unit — typically at least 10% of workers. To do this, membership data may need to be submitted to the CAC, which is an independent governmental body.

Critically, this data is not shared with Google. The CAC handles it confidentially, and the employer only sees the membership percentage.

The law forbids employers from compiling lists of union members — see Blacklisting Regulations 2010, Reg. 3.

Who can join?

Membership is open to UK-based employees of Google DeepMind. Unfortunately contractors and colleagues based outside the UK (for example in the US) aren't eligible for this recognition campaign — but US colleagues can organise with our sister union, the Alphabet Workers Union (AWU).

How much does it cost, and what do I get?

Membership is £8.70 a month. This covers the cost of running the union — trained organisers, legal advice, and support when you need it.

In return you get:

  • A vote in shaping the union's demands — the agenda is set democratically by members, not a handful of reps.
  • Access to our free legal helpline and advice from day one.
  • Individual representation in disciplinary and grievance hearings (for issues arising after your first eight weeks of membership).
  • A range of everyday member discounts — shopping, travel, insurance, gym memberships and more.

The strongest reason, though, is collective: every member makes our case for recognition stronger and the union harder to ignore.

What legal protection are we entitled to?
  • Protection from dismissal for union activity — Section 152 TULRCA 1992
  • Protection from detriment (demotion, pay cut, harassment) for union activity — Section 146 TULRCA 1992
  • Protection from dismissal for taking part in lawful industrial action (now automatically unfair, no qualifying period) — Section 238A TULRCA, as amended by Employment Rights Act 2025
  • Protection from detriment for industrial action (coming into force October 2026) — Employment Rights Act 2025, s.76 (which inserts s.236A into TULRCA 1992)
  • Right to paid time off for union reps to carry out duties — Section 168 TULRCA 1992
  • Right to unpaid time off for all members to take part in union activities — Section 170 TULRCA 1992
  • Individual representation in disciplinary and grievance hearings — Employment Relations Act 1999, s.10
  • Legal support through the union if things go wrong — Union rulebook / membership agreement
  • Democratic voice in setting demands and balloting on action — Section 226 TULRCA 1992 (ballot provisions)
  • Protection from blacklisting based on union activities — Blacklisting Regulations 2010
Why doesn't Google want us to form a union?

Together we can formally act as an employee-side counterweight to management's ability to exercise unchecked authority. Management's self-interest is to prevent us from having this kind of power.

What does statutory recognition mean for union members?

Statutory recognition gives GDM employees a legal framework to force negotiations on topics we care about if Google refuses to recognise us voluntarily. By securing recognition, we gain formal rights to collective bargaining, disclosure of internal company information, and mandatory negotiation and consultation on major workplace changes.

For statutory recognition there is a minimum membership threshold (10% of the bargaining unit, which we have passed) and all employees in the unit are called to vote. Even if you are not a union member, you can still vote — your vote will be fully anonymous. A majority vote in favour is sufficient; there is no minimum turnout threshold.

How can we extend the collective agreement beyond pay / hours / holidays?

There is no restriction on topics we can discuss in collective bargaining — including business practices, company priorities and governance. Pay, hours and holidays is the minimum legal scope, but this isn't the focus of our campaign.

To broaden the scope we need to pressure Google using our industrial leverage: unions can use actions short of a strike, campaigns, and ultimately strikes if the employer refuses. The stronger the union, the more effective the pressure. Most often, a credible threat is all it takes. Getting recognition is just the first step — then we work on extending the agreement.

I don't want to be affected by decisions made by union members — what can I do?

Decisions that affect you are already made by a much smaller pool of management executives, with no visibility or meaningful input from you: you were not consulted on the removal of the autonomous weapons or mass surveillance pledges from the AI Principles, nor on safety risks from contracts with significant human rights implications.

Collective decision-making adds democracy, not removes it. We will have a way to demand changes, and the company will have to consult us before making major decisions. Surveys can be run among all employees to better understand concerns. You will never be forced to engage or join a union.

Have you tried going through the UK Googler Forums?

We have tried to open dialogue through the UK Googler Forums, but leadership declined to discuss ethical matters through this forum. Other routes — petitions with broad support, letters, questions at townhalls — have also been tried and ignored.
